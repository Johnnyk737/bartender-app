About this recipe

Classically a 50–50 mix of gin and lime cordial, some modern Gimlet recipes tend to use lime juice for more of a fresh citric burst of flavour, while using less for a more balanced drink. Stir together and add a twist of lemon peel for added elegance. The history of the Gimlet is also the history of the British navy. In 1867 the Merchant Shipping Act made it mandatory for British ships to carry rations of limes to combat scurvy. However, it was hard to keep limes fresh over long sea journeys. So Lauchlin Rose, the owner of a shipyard in Leith, Scotland, solved it with a new product, ‘Rose’s Lime Cordial’. Sailors mixed lime cordial with rum, but the officers in the navy preferred to use gin instead, and the Gimlet was born, named after a small tool known as a gimlet that’s used to tap barrels on spirits carried on British Navy ships. It’s inspired some of the world’s best bartenders and writers. Charles H Baker celebrated the Gimlet in his 1939 book The Gentleman’s Companion as being “simple, without fancy fizzings, and is one to experiment with until the precise amount of lime cordial is found, to taste”. David Embury refers to it as a ‘Gin Rickey’ in his 1948 Fine Art of Mixing Drinks and crime writer Raymond Chandler writes of the Gimlet in The Long Goodbye as “both sweet and sharp at the same time”.

How to make

  1. Fill a glass with ice.

    Fill a mixing glass with ice cubes.

  2. Pour Gordon's London Dry Gin, lime juice and sugar into the glass.

    Using a jigger, measure 25ml Gordon's London Dry Gin, 25ml lime juice and 1tsp sugar into the glass.

  3. Stir until well combined.

    Stir the ingredients slowly with a bar spoon until well combined.

  4. Strain into a martini glass.

    Strain the mixture into a martini glass with a cocktail strainer.

  5. Garnish with a twist of lemon peel.

    Using a sharp knife and a chopping board, cut a piece of lemon peel and twist the peel onto the rim of the glass to garnish.
