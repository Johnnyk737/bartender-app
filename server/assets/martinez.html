The Martinez cocktail recipe might not have a pile of biscuits layered on top, or smoke pouring over the side, but it’s the ultimate drink – impressive in its own way. Mix up a Martinez cocktail because it has both history and flavour on its side.

About this recipe

The Martinez cocktail recipe has one great quality: it’s a sleeping giant of a drink, a little-known concoction that has actually played a major party in cocktail history. Make one up, and you’re bound to impress. And yes, that naming is no coincidence. The father of the Martini and a sibling of the Manhattan, the Martinez cocktail history dates back to New York in the 1860s. We think of all the different flavours the Martini has spawned – espresso, white chocolate – but it actually only exists because of the Martinez. So you can show all this off to your guests. But remember to do it Martinez-style: subtly. No one likes a know-it-all. Sit back and enjoy those subtle herb, fruit and botanical flavours from two vermouths plus Gordon’s London Dry Gin and a cherry liqueur. That’s quite a combo, perfect for a dinner party aperitif cocktail in announcing great cooking to come. You’re basically saying: the Martinez is all in the best possible taste – just like the food that’s on its way. So for events like a wedding party when you want to celebrate but without the showiness, the Martinez works a treat. Brash doesn’t mean better. With a Martinez it slowly becomes clear that you’re sipping a great drink. So make one up and mix with the best.

How to make

  1. Add ice to a glass.

    Half-fill a glass with cubes of ice.

  2. Add in Gordon’s London Dry Gin, red vermouth, maraschino liqueur and angostura aromatic bitters.

    Using a jigger, measure 50ml Gordon’s London Dry Gin, 10ml red vermouth, 5ml maraschino liqueur and a dash of angostura aromatic bitters into the glass.

  3. Stir thoroughly.

    Place a barspoon at the bottom of the glass and gently turn to combine all ingredients.

  4. Strain into a chilled glass.

    Pour the liquid through a cocktail strainer or small sieve into a coupe glass that’s been chilled in the fridge.

  5. Garnish with orange peel.

    Using a small knife or vegetable peeler, cut a think piece of peel from one turn of an orange. Place on top of the drink to garnish.

Avoid top-ups

It’s easier to keep track of your drinks if you know how many full glasses you’re drinking.
